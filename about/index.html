Welcome to my programming blog.

Hey guy, what’s with the name?

“What’s a layman programmer?” you ask? Well, that’s me - or at least what I feel is a suitable description of myself. I know, humble brag. “Care to elaborate on that?” Sure. I graduated from the University of Connecticut in 2012 with a BA in Actuarial Science.

If you’re familiar with Actuarial Science, that’s great - you would have saved me a slightly tedious, several minute long explanation in a face-to-face conversation. If not, don’t worry, you’re not alone. Just throwing out a rough estimate, I’d say that maybe 1 in 3 people know what an actuary actually does (pardon the alliteration…) - and by know I mean something besides “Oh, like the guy in that Ben Stiller movie, right??”. Anyways, a perfect segue to my next humble brag, right? I’ll resist the urge just this once, and direct you to the Wiki page linked above for more details.

“So getting back to your imaginary my question…” Right, sorry. Getting back to the point, my academic background is in mathematics, not computer science. In fact, up until my final semester of college I didn’t know a lick of programming, as my only prior encounter with code was Introductory Visual Basic in 9th grade. Of course, the little bit I might have learned in that class had long since faded - my sole takeaway being that some instructors take variable naming conventions very seriously way too seriously for a freshman highschool classroom. But in the Spring semester of my senior year I took a great course on Actuarial Statistics to fulfill a VEE requirement - and was fortunate to have a fantastic professor whose curriculum involved a lot of hands-on work with the R programming language.

I certainly was not an R guru in any sense of the word by the end of that semester (or even by now, for that matter), but it was a valuable experience nonetheless, leading me down a rabbit hole into the vast universe of programming. Putting things more concretely, I write this passage in plain text using my editor of choice, Vim, on a laptop that dual boots two flavors of Linux, CentOS and Ubuntu - from a SSD that I swapped the factory hard disk for - which by some sort of magic (re: the much appreciated time, effort, and ingenuity invested by numerous individuals who felt compelled to give back to the open-source community) will be transformed into the colored, stylized, formatted, static webpage you now see, simply by issuing a quick rake generate and rake deploy.

“You seem to just be patting yourself on the back. Is there a point to all this?” Yes and yes. The point is this: three years ago, I hadn’t heard of any of those things. I had no concept of GitHub, Sublime Text, Hacker News, or hashbangs. yum? apt-get? What the hell is that? Linus who? Scott Meyers? Nope, never heard of them. Now these${tools,software,people} are part of everyday life (well, not so much the latter, but you get what I’m saying…). I didn’t learn any SQL, C++, or Bash in a classroom. Instrumental as it was, 99% of the R code I write during my nine-to-five wasn’t acquired in my actuarial stats course. That’s not a complaint, mind you - being a math major wasn’t a walk in the park, but I loved it then, and still do. Yet on any given day, I spend much more time programming than solving equations. In reality, most of the tangible skills that I get paid for have been picked up from blogs, books purchased from Amazon, StackOverflow, or this one guy I work with who seems to know a little a lot about everything. As someone who had never even heard the term “regex” not that long ago, having a coworker with a strong Perl background is truly a blessing, trust me. Occasionally I reflect on this, and wonder how it’s possible for perspectives, goals, daily routines, interests, etc.. to change so dramatically in a few years. Then I remember that the internet is big fucking huge. There’s a lot of knowledge floating around out there - and it’s basically free. And in the so called Age of Information, I’d be crazy to not learn a few lines of code, right? Right.

“Sooo…” So, wrapping it up, this blog is mainly about things I’ve picked up from my day-to-day programming experiences, things I actually use on the job (hint: I don’t work for Google, or Apple, or anywhere crazy like that). But as always, if you see something incorrect, or think you have a better approach, feel free to contribute.
